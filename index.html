Options, Gamma & GEX · a visual guide

How options quietly move the stock market

You have probably heard traders talk about gamma squeezes, 0DTE, or "dealers are short gamma". This page builds those ideas from zero — what an option is, what delta and gamma mean, and how the hedging done by option dealers (GEX, gamma exposure) can calm the market on some days and amplify it on others. 1What is an option?

An option is a contract on a stock. A call gives you the right (not the obligation) to buy the stock at a fixed price — the strike — before an expiry date. A put gives you the right to sell at the strike. You pay a premium up front for that right. One contract covers 100 shares; the charts here show per-share dollars to keep the numbers small.

The classic way to see an option is its payoff at expiry: for every possible stock price on expiry day, what did you make or lose?

Profit / loss at expiry (per share)

Try it: buy a call with strike $100 and premium $5. Below $100 you lose exactly the $5 premium — never more. Above $105 (the breakeven, marked on the chart) you profit dollar-for-dollar. Now flip to Sell: the picture mirrors — the seller keeps the premium in the flat zone but has open-ended risk on the other side. That seller is usually a dealer, and their risk is what the rest of this page is about.

Key idea

Options have asymmetric payoffs: limited loss on one side, open-ended exposure on the other. Whoever sells you that asymmetry has to manage it — by trading the stock itself.

2What is an option worth before expiry?

Before expiry, an option is worth more than its immediate exercise value, because the stock still has time to move. The market prices this with models like Black–Scholes, which turn time-to-expiry and expected wobble (implied volatility, IV) into a smooth curve. The gap between the curve and the kinked expiry payoff is time value.

Call option value vs. stock price (strike $100)

Try it: drag days to expiry down to 1. The smooth curve collapses onto the kink — time value melts away (that decay is “theta”). Now push IV up: more expected movement makes every option worth more, especially near the strike.

3Delta: the option’s “share equivalent”

Delta is the slope of the value curve you just saw: how much the option’s price changes when the stock moves $1. A call with delta 0.50 behaves, for small moves, like holding 50 shares per contract. Deltas run from 0 to 1 for calls and 0 to −1 for puts.

Delta vs. stock price (strike $100)

Try it: shrink days to expiry. The call’s delta curve steepens into a step: far from the strike an option is either “certainly worthless” (delta 0) or “certainly stock” (delta 1), and near the strike it flips faster and faster. That steepness is gamma — the next section.

Why delta matters for the market

Anyone who sold you an option can neutralize their directional risk by holding delta × 100 shares against each contract. This is called delta hedging, and dealers do it constantly. The catch: delta doesn’t sit still.

4Gamma: how fast delta changes

Gamma is the rate of change of delta per $1 move in the stock. High gamma means a dealer’s hedge goes stale quickly — every move in the stock forces them to re-trade. Gamma is largest at the strike (where the “maybe” is most uncertain) and explodes as expiry approaches.

Gamma vs. stock price (strike $100, IV 25%)

Gamma = change in delta per $1 stock move · same option, three expiries

Read it: with 30 days left, gamma is a gentle hill. With 1 day left it is a spike: near the strike, a $1 move can swing delta by 0.20 or more. This is why expiry days (and 0DTE options) generate so much forced hedging — tiny stock moves demand big hedge adjustments.

The two regimes

Direction is everything. A dealer who is short options (short gamma) hedges with the market — buying rallies, selling dips — which amplifies moves. A dealer who is long options (long gamma) hedges against the market — selling rallies, buying dips — which dampens moves. To know which force dominates, we add up gamma across all options that exist. That sum is GEX.

6GEX: gamma exposure, added up

GEX (gamma exposure) estimates, for every strike, how many dollars of stock dealers must trade per 1% market move, given the open interest (contracts outstanding) at that strike. One problem: nobody publishes which side dealers are actually on. So the standard quick estimate assumes typical customer behavior — investors like to sell covered calls for income and buy protective puts for insurance. Dealers take the other side of both, ending up long calls (positive gamma) and short puts (negative gamma). It’s a crude assumption, but it makes a useful map:

Try it: drag the price down toward the put-heavy strikes. Total net GEX flips negative — the market has crossed the gamma flip level into the unstable regime. Drag it back up among the call strikes and net GEX turns positive again. Real GEX dashboards (published daily by several research shops) show exactly this picture for the S&P 500.

How to read a GEX chart

Above the flip (net GEX positive): dealers are long gamma — their hedging leans against moves, volatility gets suppressed, price tends to “pin” near big strikes. Below the flip (net GEX negative): dealers are short gamma — hedging chases moves, selling begets selling, and volatility expands. That’s why the flip level is watched like a tripwire.

8Where you’ve seen this in the wild

🚀 The gamma squeeze (GameStop, January 2021)

Crowds bought huge volumes of short-dated out-of-the-money calls. Dealers who sold them were short gamma and had to buy shares as the price rose — which pushed the price higher, raising the calls’ deltas, forcing more buying. The hedging loop itself became the rally. That feedback spiral is a gamma squeeze.

📌 OPEX pinning

On big monthly expiration days (“OPEX”), strikes with heavy open interest act like magnets when dealers are long gamma: every drift away from the strike triggers hedging that nudges the price back. Markets often go strangely quiet and “pin” to round numbers like 100 — then move freely again once the options expire and the gamma vanishes.

⚡ The 0DTE era

Options expiring the same day (zero days to expiry) now make up a large share of S&P 500 option volume. Remember the 1-day gamma spike from section 4: 0DTE options carry enormous gamma concentrated at the money, so intraday hedging flows have become a bigger driver of intraday market behavior than ever.

🗺️ Where people watch this

Several research services publish daily GEX estimates and gamma-flip levels for indexes and single stocks (e.g. SqueezeMetrics’ GEX, SpotGamma, Menthor Q, and free community dashboards). They all rest on the same naive dealer-positioning assumption from section 6 — useful as a map, not as gospel.

Glossary

Call / Put
The right to buy (call) or sell (put) 100 shares at the strike price, until expiry.
Strike
The fixed price the option lets you trade at.
Premium
What you pay for the option.
Expiry / DTE
When the contract ends; DTE = days to expiry. 0DTE = expires today.
ATM / ITM / OTM
At/in/out of the money — stock near / past / short of the strike.
Implied volatility (IV)
The amount of future movement the option’s price implies.
Delta
Option price change per $1 stock move; the option’s share-equivalent.
Gamma
Delta’s change per $1 stock move; how fast a hedge goes stale.
Theta
Daily decay of an option’s time value.
Market maker / dealer
The professional counterparty on most option trades; hedges rather than bets.
Delta hedging
Holding delta × 100 shares per contract to neutralize direction.
Open interest (OI)
Number of contracts outstanding at a strike.
GEX (gamma exposure)
Estimated dollars dealers must trade per 1% market move, summed across strikes.
Gamma flip
The price level where net GEX changes sign — the boundary between the calm and unstable regimes.
Gamma squeeze
A self-reinforcing rally driven by dealers hedging short calls.
OPEX
Monthly options expiration day, when large gamma positions expire at once.
Leg
One option (or share position) inside a multi-part strategy.
Spread
Buying one option and selling another against it. Debit spreads cost money up front; credit spreads collect it.
Straddle / strangle
A call and a put bought together — same strike (straddle) or different strikes (strangle) — a bet on movement, not direction.
Butterfly
A three-strike combo (buy–sell 2×–buy) that pays most if the stock closes at the middle strike; the capped bet on a pin.
Iron condor
A short put spread plus a short call spread; keeps its credit while the stock stays inside the band.
Honest caveats

Everything here is a teaching model. Black–Scholes assumes constant volatility and no jumps; naive GEX assumes it knows which side dealers are on (they publish no such thing); the regime simulator compresses continuous intraday hedging into a one-day feedback term. Directionally right, numerically simplified — and none of it is investment advice.

How the premium is estimated

There is no free archive of historical option prices, so premiums (every leg of a strategy) are estimated with Black–Scholes from the stock’s realized volatility over the prior 3 months — with a +35% markup on earnings scenarios, since options are always pricier before a report. Real premiums differed, sometimes a lot. The stock prices and earnings dates themselves are real (split-adjusted).
